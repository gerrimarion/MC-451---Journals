## Your Reflection

While reading the final readings for the semester, I thought about what was mentioned in Chapter 19, leading into the data interpreting and finalizing phase. I heard this specific phrase a lot my first year when I took a Computer Concepts class, which was mostly us working in Excel. "Correlation does not mean causation."

Just because something *looks* like it's happening doesn't mean that it is. If you look up different correlation charts at first glance, they're fairly startling. I remember one being shown in class that involved the purchase of avocados and the number of car accidents that year. Since they were so similar on a line graph, there would be an initial assumption that there's something going on. 

Until you think about it and realize that it doesn't make a whole lot of sense. Sure, it could be something to look into, but is very unlikely to be happening. Correlation does not mean causation, and there needs to be a level of awareness to understand that. 

As for my own research, I except a fairly null hypothesis and outcome. I'm working with a relatively small and specific dataset so I don't expect to see a whole lot of insane differences overall. However, I will be keeping in mind that if a correlation exists, it doesn't mean that there is a causation. I could maybe be a theme, but there's never a 100% surety in anything. I might see some things because I did notice that there was gender gratification when it came to verbal responses while playing the chosen videogames, but again, that doesn't mean they're a cause for that reaction. There may be a correlation to things, but certainly not a cause to them.
